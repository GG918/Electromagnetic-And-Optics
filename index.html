Chapter 1: Basic Concepts and Mathematical Background

1. Vector Analysis

Vector Operations:

  • Addition and Subtraction: Combine vectors component-wise.
  • Dot Product (•): $\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = |\vec{A}||\vec{B}|\cos\theta$
  • Cross Product (×): $\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = |\vec{A}||\vec{B}|\sin\theta \hat{n}$

2. Coordinate Systems

Cartesian (Rectangular) Coordinates (x, y, z):

  • Suitable for problems with rectangular symmetry.
  • Differential elements: $dx, dy, dz$

Cylindrical Coordinates (ρ, φ, z):

  • Used for problems with cylindrical symmetry.
  • Relationships: $x = \rho \cos\phi$
    $y = \rho \sin\phi$
  • Differential elements: $d\rho, \rho d\phi, dz$

Spherical Coordinates (r, θ, φ):

  • Ideal for problems with spherical symmetry.
  • Relationships: $x = r \sin\theta \cos\phi$
    $y = r \sin\theta \sin\phi$
    $z = r \cos\theta$
  • Differential elements: $dr, r d\theta, r \sin\theta d\phi$

3. Differential Operators

Gradient (∇f):

$\nabla f = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\hat{i} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\hat{j} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\hat{k}$

Divergence (∇⋅A):

$\nabla \cdot \vec{A} = \frac{\partial A_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial A_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial A_z}{\partial z}$

Curl (∇×A):

$\nabla \times \vec{A} = \left(\frac{\partial A_z}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial A_y}{\partial z}\right)\hat{i} + \left(\frac{\partial A_x}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial A_z}{\partial x}\right)\hat{j} + \left(\frac{\partial A_y}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial A_x}{\partial y}\right)\hat{k}$

Laplacian (∇²f):

$\nabla^2 f = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial z^2}$

4. Fundamental Theorems of Vector Calculus

Gauss's Divergence Theorem:

$\oint_S \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{S} = \int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{A}) dV$

Stokes's Theorem:

$\oint_C \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \vec{A}) \cdot d\vec{S}$

5. Maxwell's Equations (Overview)

Gauss's Law for Electricity:

Differential Form: $\nabla \cdot \vec{D} = \rho_v$

Integral Form: $\oint_S \vec{D} \cdot d\vec{S} = \int_V \rho_v dV$

Gauss's Law for Magnetism:

Differential Form: $\nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0$

Integral Form: $\oint_S \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{S} = 0$

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction:

Differential Form: $\nabla \times \vec{E} = -\frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}$

Integral Form: $\oint_C \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} = -\int_S \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \cdot d\vec{S}$

Ampère's Law with Maxwell's Addition:

Differential Form: $\nabla \times \vec{H} = \vec{J} + \frac{\partial \vec{D}}{\partial t}$

Integral Form: $\oint_C \vec{H} \cdot d\vec{l} = \int_S (\vec{J} + \frac{\partial \vec{D}}{\partial t}) \cdot d\vec{S}$

6. Boundary Conditions

At the Interface Between Two Media:

Electric Fields:

  • Tangential Component: $E_{1t} = E_{2t}$
  • Normal Component: $D_{1n} - D_{2n} = \rho_s$

Magnetic Fields:

  • Tangential Component: $H_{1t} - H_{2t} = \vec{K_s} \times \hat{n}$
  • Normal Component: $B_{1n} = B_{2n}$

7. Material Properties

Permittivity (ε):

$\vec{D} = \varepsilon \vec{E}$

Permeability (μ):

$\vec{B} = \mu \vec{H}$

Conductivity (σ):

$\vec{J} = \sigma \vec{E}$

8. Time-Varying Fields and Displacement Current

Displacement Current Density (∂D/∂t):

Introduced to modify Ampère's Law to include time-varying electric fields.

Ensures continuity in the presence of capacitors and varying fields.

Continuity Equation:

$\nabla \cdot \vec{J} + \frac{\partial \rho_v}{\partial t} = 0$

9. Wave Equations

Derived from Maxwell's equations for regions where there are no free charges or currents.

Electric Field Wave Equation:

$\nabla^2 \vec{E} - \mu\varepsilon\frac{\partial^2 \vec{E}}{\partial t^2} = 0$

Magnetic Field Wave Equation:

$\nabla^2 \vec{H} - \mu\varepsilon\frac{\partial^2 \vec{H}}{\partial t^2} = 0$

Describe the propagation of electromagnetic waves in a medium.

Chapter 2: Maxwell's Equations, Constitutive Relations, and Boundary Conditions

1. Introduction to Maxwell's Equations

Maxwell's equations are the foundational laws of electromagnetism, describing how electric and magnetic fields are generated and altered by each other and by charges and currents. They consist of four equations, which can be expressed in both integral and differential forms.

Gauss's Law for Electricity

Integral Form: $\oint_S \vec{D} \cdot d\vec{S} = \int_V \rho_v dV$

Differential Form: $\nabla \cdot \vec{D} = \rho_v$

Gauss's Law for Magnetism

Integral Form: $\oint_S \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{S} = 0$

Differential Form: $\nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0$

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction

Integral Form: $\oint_C \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} = -\int_S \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \cdot d\vec{S}$

Differential Form: $\nabla \times \vec{E} = -\frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}$

Ampère's Law with Maxwell's Addition

Integral Form: $\oint_C \vec{H} \cdot d\vec{l} = \int_S (\vec{J} + \frac{\partial \vec{D}}{\partial t}) \cdot d\vec{S}$

Differential Form: $\nabla \times \vec{H} = \vec{J} + \frac{\partial \vec{D}}{\partial t}$

2. Constitutive Relations

Electric Field and Electric Flux Density

Relation: $\vec{D} = \varepsilon \vec{E}$

ε: Permittivity of the material.

Magnetic Field Intensity and Magnetic Flux Density

Relation: $\vec{B} = \mu \vec{H}$

μ: Permeability of the material.

Current Density and Electric Field

Ohm's Law in Point Form: $\vec{J} = \sigma \vec{E}$

σ: Conductivity of the material.

3. Maxwell's Equations in Material Media

Combining Maxwell's equations with the constitutive relations gives the equations specific to materials.

Maxwell's Equations in Homogeneous, Isotropic Media are simplified when ε, μ, σ are constants.

4. The Continuity Equation

Ensures the conservation of electric charge.

Equation: $\nabla \cdot \vec{J} + \frac{\partial \rho_v}{\partial t} = 0$

5. Derivation of the Wave Equations

By manipulating Maxwell's equations, wave equations for the electric and magnetic fields can be derived.

Electric Field Wave Equation

Equation: $\nabla^2 \vec{E} - \mu\varepsilon\frac{\partial^2 \vec{E}}{\partial t^2} = \mu\sigma\frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} + \nabla(\nabla \cdot \vec{E})$

In Lossless, Source-Free, Homogeneous Media: $\nabla^2 \vec{E} - \mu\varepsilon\frac{\partial^2 \vec{E}}{\partial t^2} = 0$

Magnetic Field Wave Equation

Equation: $\nabla^2 \vec{H} - \mu\varepsilon\frac{\partial^2 \vec{H}}{\partial t^2} = \mu\sigma\frac{\partial \vec{H}}{\partial t} + \nabla \times (\vec{J_s})$

In Lossless, Source-Free, Homogeneous Media: $\nabla^2 \vec{H} - \mu\varepsilon\frac{\partial^2 \vec{H}}{\partial t^2} = 0$

6. Plane Electromagnetic Waves

Definition

Solutions to the wave equations representing waves with constant amplitude and phase fronts that are planes perpendicular to the direction of propagation.

Uniform Plane Waves

Electric and Magnetic Fields:

$\vec{E}(\vec{r},t) = \vec{E_0}e^{j(\omega t - \vec{k}\cdot\vec{r})}$

$\vec{H}(\vec{r},t) = \vec{H_0}e^{j(\omega t - \vec{k}\cdot\vec{r})}$

$\vec{E_0}, \vec{H_0}$: Amplitudes of the fields.

ω: Angular frequency.

$\vec{k}$: Wave vector (direction and magnitude of propagation).

Wave Propagation Parameters

Wave Number (k): $k = \omega\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon}$

Phase Velocity (vp): $v_p = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon}}$

Intrinsic Impedance (η): $\eta = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\varepsilon}}$

Relation between E and H: $\vec{H} = \frac{1}{\eta}(\hat{k} \times \vec{E})$

$\hat{k}$: Unit vector in the direction of propagation.

7. Polarization of Electromagnetic Waves

Describes the orientation of the electric field vector as the wave propagates.

Types of Polarization

  • Linear Polarization: Electric field vector maintains a constant direction.
  • Circular Polarization: Electric field vector rotates in a circle as the wave propagates; magnitude remains constant.
  • Elliptical Polarization: General case where the tip of the electric field vector traces an ellipse.

8. Energy and Power in Electromagnetic Fields

Poynting Vector

Definition: $\vec{S} = \vec{E} \times \vec{H}$

Poynting's Theorem

Energy Conservation Law: $\nabla \cdot \vec{S} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \vec{J} \cdot \vec{E} = 0$

u: Energy density of the electromagnetic field.

$u = \frac{1}{2}(\vec{E} \cdot \vec{D} + \vec{B} \cdot \vec{H})$

9. Boundary Conditions Revisited

Building upon Chapter 1, further details on boundary conditions for electromagnetic fields at interfaces between different media are provided.

Reflection and Transmission at Normal Incidence

Reflection Coefficient (Γ): $\Gamma = \frac{\eta_2 - \eta_1}{\eta_2 + \eta_1}$

Transmission Coefficient (τ): $\tau = \frac{2\eta_2}{\eta_2 + \eta_1}$

$\eta_1, \eta_2$: Intrinsic impedances of media 1 and 2.

Snell's Law and Refraction

Law of Refraction: $\frac{\sin \theta_1}{\sin \theta_2} = \frac{v_{p1}}{v_{p2}} = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_1\varepsilon_1}{\mu_2\varepsilon_2}}$

$\theta_1, \theta_2$: Angles of incidence and refraction.

10. Complex Permittivity and Permeability

In conductive or lossy materials, permittivity and permeability can be complex to account for losses.

Complex Permittivity (εc):

Definition: $\varepsilon_c = \varepsilon - j\frac{\sigma}{\omega}$

σ: Conductivity.

j: Imaginary unit.

Attenuation and Phase Constants

Propagation Constant (γ): $\gamma = \alpha + j\beta$

α: Attenuation constant.

β: Phase constant.

Expressions:

$\alpha = \omega\sqrt{\frac{\mu\varepsilon}{2}} [\sqrt{1 + (\frac{\sigma}{\omega\varepsilon})^2} - 1]^{1/2}$

$\beta = \omega\sqrt{\frac{\mu\varepsilon}{2}} [\sqrt{1 + (\frac{\sigma}{\omega\varepsilon})^2} + 1]^{1/2}$

These constants describe how the amplitude and phase of the wave change as it propagates through a lossy medium.

11. Skin Effect

Describes how alternating current (AC) tends to flow near the surface of a conductor at high frequencies.

Skin Depth (δ):

$\delta = \frac{1}{\alpha} = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\omega\mu\sigma}}$

Chapter 3: Transmission Line Theory

1. Introduction to Transmission Lines

Transmission lines are structures that guide electromagnetic waves from one point to another, such as coaxial cables, waveguides, and microstrip lines. Understanding transmission lines is essential for the analysis and design of high-frequency communication systems, antennas, and microwave circuits.

2. Transmission Line Parameters

Transmission lines are characterized by four primary distributed parameters per unit length:

  • Resistance (R): Represents the conductor's resistance, causing power loss due to heat.
  • Inductance (L): Accounts for the magnetic field around the conductor.
  • Conductance (G): Represents the dielectric losses in the insulating material between conductors.
  • Capacitance (C): Accounts for the electric field between conductors.

These parameters are used to model the transmission line as a distributed network of infinitesimal circuit elements.

3. Transmission Line Equations

The voltage V(z,t) and current I(z,t) along the transmission line satisfy the telegrapher's equations:

Voltage Equation:

$\frac{\partial V(z,t)}{\partial z} = -(R + j\omega L)I(z,t)$

Current Equation:

$\frac{\partial I(z,t)}{\partial z} = -(G + j\omega C)V(z,t)$

ω: Angular frequency (ω = 2πf)

j: Imaginary unit ($\sqrt{-1}$)

z: Position along the transmission line

4. Wave Equations on Transmission Lines

By combining the telegrapher's equations, we obtain second-order differential equations for voltage and current:

Voltage Wave Equation:

$\frac{\partial^2 V(z)}{\partial z^2} = \gamma^2V(z)$

Current Wave Equation:

$\frac{\partial^2 I(z)}{\partial z^2} = \gamma^2I(z)$

γ: Propagation constant, defined as:

$\gamma = \alpha + j\beta = \sqrt{(R + j\omega L)(G + j\omega C)}$

α: Attenuation constant (nepers per unit length)

β: Phase constant (radians per unit length)

5. Solutions to the Wave Equations

The general solutions for voltage and current along the transmission line are:

Voltage:

$V(z) = V^+e^{-\gamma z} + V^-e^{\gamma z}$

Current:

$I(z) = \frac{V^+}{Z_0}e^{-\gamma z} - \frac{V^-}{Z_0}e^{\gamma z}$

$V^+$: Amplitude of the forward (traveling in the +z direction) voltage wave

$V^-$: Amplitude of the backward (traveling in the -z direction) voltage wave

$Z_0$: Characteristic impedance of the line

6. Characteristic Impedance ($Z_0$)

The characteristic impedance is a fundamental property of the transmission line, defined as:

$Z_0 = \sqrt{\frac{R + j\omega L}{G + j\omega C}}$

For lossless lines (R = 0, G = 0):

$Z_0 = \sqrt{\frac{L}{C}}$

7. Propagation Constant and Wave Parameters

For lossless lines, the propagation constant simplifies to:

Attenuation Constant (α):

$\alpha = 0$ (no loss)

Phase Constant (β):

$\beta = \omega\sqrt{LC}$

Phase Velocity ($v_p$):

$v_p = \frac{\omega}{\beta} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$

8. Reflection and Transmission Coefficients

When a transmission line is terminated with a load impedance ($Z_L$), reflections occur unless $Z_L = Z_0$.

Reflection Coefficient at the Load ($\Gamma_L$):

$\Gamma_L = \frac{Z_L - Z_0}{Z_L + Z_0}$

Voltage at Any Point on the Line:

$V(z) = V_0^+(e^{-j\beta z} + \Gamma_Le^{j\beta z})$

$V_0^+$: Incident voltage amplitude at z = 0

9. Standing Waves and Standing Wave Ratio (SWR)

Due to reflections, standing waves are formed along the line.

Voltage Standing Wave Ratio (VSWR or SWR):

$VSWR = \frac{V_{max}}{V_{min}} = \frac{1 + |\Gamma_L|}{1 - |\Gamma_L|}$

$V_{max}, V_{min}$: Maximum and minimum voltage amplitudes along the line

10. Input Impedance of the Transmission Line

The input impedance at a distance l from the load is:

$Z_{in}(l) = Z_0\frac{Z_L + Z_0\tanh(\gamma l)}{Z_0 + Z_L\tanh(\gamma l)}$

For lossless lines ($\gamma = j\beta$):

$Z_{in}(l) = Z_0\frac{Z_L + jZ_0\tan(\beta l)}{Z_0 + jZ_L\tan(\beta l)}$

11. Special Cases

Short-Circuited Line ($Z_L = 0$):

$Z_{in}(l) = jZ_0\tan(\beta l)$

Open-Circuited Line ($Z_L \to \infty$):

$Z_{in}(l) = -jZ_0\cot(\beta l)$

12. Impedance Matching

To maximize power transfer and minimize reflections, impedance matching is essential.

Quarter-Wave Transformer:

A λ/4 (quarter-wavelength) section of transmission line can match a load impedance to the line.

Characteristic Impedance of the Transformer ($Z_{0t}$):

$Z_{0t} = \sqrt{Z_0Z_L}$

13. Smith Chart

The Smith Chart is a graphical tool used to solve transmission line problems involving complex impedances.

Features:

  • Represents normalized impedance and admittance.
  • Circles of constant resistance and reactance.
  • Enables visualization of impedance transformations along the line.

14. Power Considerations

Instantaneous Power:

$p(z,t) = \text{Re}[V(z)e^{j\omega t}] \times \text{Re}[I^*(z)e^{-j\omega t}]$

Average Power:

$P_{avg} = \frac{1}{2}\text{Re}[V(z)I^*(z)]$

$I^*(z)$: Complex conjugate of the current

15. Lossy Transmission Lines

For lines with non-negligible R and G:

Attenuation Constant (α):

$\alpha = \text{Re}[\gamma]$

Phase Constant (β):

$\beta = \text{Im}[\gamma]$

Approximate Expressions (when $R \ll \omega L$ and $G \ll \omega C$):

$\alpha \approx \frac{R}{2Z_0} + \frac{GZ_0}{2}$

$\beta \approx \omega\sqrt{LC}$

16. Distortionless Line

A transmission line is distortionless if $R/L = G/C$.

Condition for No Signal Distortion:

  • The attenuation constant α is frequency-independent.
  • The phase constant β varies linearly with frequency.

Characteristic Impedance and Propagation Constant:

$Z_0 = \sqrt{\frac{R + j\omega L}{G + j\omega C}} = \sqrt{\frac{L}{C}}$

$\gamma = \sqrt{RG} + j\omega\sqrt{LC}$

17. Transient Analysis

When signals change abruptly, transient analysis is necessary.

Reflections due to Discontinuities:

  • Transients occur when switching or when there is a mismatch in impedance.
  • The voltage and current can be analyzed using reflection diagrams or the method of characteristics.

18. Applications

  • Communication Systems: Transmission lines are used in RF and microwave communications to transmit signals with minimal loss and distortion.
  • Antenna Feed Lines: Impedance matching is critical to ensure efficient power transfer between antennas and transmitters/receivers.
  • High-Speed Digital Circuits: Transmission line effects become significant at high frequencies, affecting signal integrity.

Chapter 4: Wave Propagation in Unbounded Media

1. Introduction to Electromagnetic Wave Propagation

Electromagnetic waves propagate through different media, and understanding their behavior in unbounded (infinite) media is fundamental. This chapter explores how waves travel in free space, dielectrics, and conductors, emphasizing the mathematical descriptions and physical interpretations.

2. Uniform Plane Waves

Definition

Uniform Plane Wave: A wave whose electric (E) and magnetic (H) field vectors are uniform (constant magnitude and direction) over any plane perpendicular to the direction of propagation.

Mathematical Representation

Electric Field: $\vec{E}(\vec{r},t) = \vec{E_0}e^{j(\omega t - \vec{k}\cdot\vec{r})}$

Magnetic Field: $\vec{H}(\vec{r},t) = \vec{H_0}e^{j(\omega t - \vec{k}\cdot\vec{r})}$

$\vec{E_0}, \vec{H_0}$: Amplitudes of the fields.

ω: Angular frequency.

$\vec{k}$: Wave vector, $\vec{k} = \hat{n}k$, where $\hat{n}$ is the unit propagation vector.

Wave Parameters

Wave Number (k): $k = \omega\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon}$

Phase Velocity ($v_p$): $v_p = \frac{\omega}{k} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon}}$

Intrinsic Impedance (η): $\eta = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\varepsilon}}$

Relates the electric and magnetic fields: $\vec{H} = \frac{1}{\eta}(\hat{k} \times \vec{E})$

3. Propagation in Lossless Dielectric Media

Properties

Lossless Medium: Perfect dielectric with no conductivity (σ = 0).

Propagation Constant (γ): $\gamma = j\beta = j\omega\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon}$

Attenuation Constant (α): $\alpha = 0$ (no loss).

Wave Equations

Electric Field: $\nabla^2\vec{E} = \mu\varepsilon\frac{\partial^2\vec{E}}{\partial t^2}$

Magnetic Field: $\nabla^2\vec{H} = \mu\varepsilon\frac{\partial^2\vec{H}}{\partial t^2}$

4. Propagation in Lossy Dielectric Media

Properties

Conductive Medium: Dielectric with finite conductivity (σ ≠ 0).

Complex Permittivity ($\varepsilon_c$): $\varepsilon_c = \varepsilon - j\frac{\sigma}{\omega}$

Propagation Constant (γ): $\gamma = \alpha + j\beta = j\omega\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon_c}$

Attenuation and Phase Constants

Attenuation Constant (α): $\alpha = \omega\sqrt{\frac{\mu\varepsilon}{2}}[\sqrt{1+(\frac{\sigma}{\omega\varepsilon})^2}-1]^{1/2}$

Phase Constant (β): $\beta = \omega\sqrt{\frac{\mu\varepsilon}{2}}[\sqrt{1+(\frac{\sigma}{\omega\varepsilon})^2}+1]^{1/2}$

Skin Depth (δ)

Definition: $\delta = \frac{1}{\alpha}$

Interpretation: Distance over which the wave amplitude decreases to 1/e of its value at the surface.

5. Propagation in Good Conductors

High Conductivity Approximation

For good conductors (σ ≫ ωε):

Propagation Constant: $\gamma \approx (1+j)\sqrt{\frac{\omega\mu\sigma}{2}}$

Attenuation Constant: $\alpha \approx \beta \approx \sqrt{\pi f\mu\sigma}$

Implications

  • Rapid Attenuation: Electromagnetic waves are heavily attenuated in good conductors.
  • Surface Currents: Currents are confined near the surface due to the skin effect.

6. Polarization of Electromagnetic Waves

Types of Polarization

  • Linear Polarization: Electric field vector maintains a constant direction.
  • Circular Polarization: Electric field rotates in a circle, magnitude remains constant.
  • Elliptical Polarization: General case where the tip of the electric field vector traces an ellipse.

Mathematical Representation

Electric Field Components: $\vec{E} = \hat{x}E_{x0}e^{j(\omega t-kz+\phi_x)} + \hat{y}E_{y0}e^{j(\omega t-kz+\phi_y)}$

$E_{x0}, E_{y0}$: Amplitudes along x and y axes.

$\phi_x, \phi_y$: Phase angles.

7. Reflection and Transmission at Planar Boundaries

Normal Incidence

Reflection Coefficient (Γ): $\Gamma = \frac{\eta_2 - \eta_1}{\eta_2 + \eta_1}$

Transmission Coefficient (T): $T = \frac{2\eta_2}{\eta_2 + \eta_1}$

$\eta_1, \eta_2$: Intrinsic impedances of media 1 and 2.

Oblique Incidence

Snell's Law: $\frac{\sin \theta_i}{\sin \theta_t} = \frac{v_{p1}}{v_{p2}} = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_1\varepsilon_1}{\mu_2\varepsilon_2}}$

$\theta_i$: Angle of incidence.

$\theta_t$: Angle of transmission (refraction).

Critical Angle and Total Internal Reflection:

Occurs when $\theta_t = 90°$, leading to:

$\theta_c = \sin^{-1}(\frac{v_{p2}}{v_{p1}})$

Fresnel Equations

Parallel (p) Polarization:

Reflection Coefficient: $\Gamma_p = \frac{\eta_2\cos \theta_i - \eta_1\cos \theta_t}{\eta_2\cos \theta_i + \eta_1\cos \theta_t}$

Perpendicular (s) Polarization:

Reflection Coefficient: $\Gamma_s = \frac{\eta_1\cos \theta_i - \eta_2\cos \theta_t}{\eta_1\cos \theta_i + \eta_2\cos \theta_t}$

8. Brewster's Angle

Definition: The angle of incidence at which there is no reflection for the parallel polarization ($\Gamma_p = 0$).

Expression: $\theta_B = \tan^{-1}(\frac{\eta_2}{\eta_1})$

9. Poynting Vector and Power Flow

Poynting Vector

Definition: $\vec{S} = \vec{E} \times \vec{H}^*$

$\vec{H}^*$: Complex conjugate of the magnetic field.

Average Power Density: $\langle\vec{S}\rangle = \frac{1}{2}\text{Re}[\vec{E} \times \vec{H}^*]$

Power Transmission and Reflection

Transmitted Power Density: $\langle\vec{S_t}\rangle = |T|^2\langle\vec{S_i}\rangle$

Reflected Power Density: $\langle\vec{S_r}\rangle = |\Gamma|^2\langle\vec{S_i}\rangle$

$\langle\vec{S_i}\rangle$: Incident power density.

10. Electromagnetic Wave Equation Solutions

General Solution in Homogeneous Media

Wave Equation: $\nabla^2\vec{E} - \mu\varepsilon\frac{\partial^2\vec{E}}{\partial t^2} = 0$

Plane Wave Solutions: $\vec{E}(\vec{r},t) = \vec{E_0}e^{j(\omega t-\vec{k}\cdot\vec{r})}$

Boundary Conditions

At Interfaces:

  • Tangential Components of E and H are continuous across boundaries unless there are surface charges or currents.

11. Dispersion and Group Velocity

Dispersion

Definition: Variation of phase velocity with frequency due to material properties.

Dispersion Relation: $\omega = \omega(k)$

Group Velocity ($v_g$)

Definition: $v_g = \frac{d\omega}{dk}$

Physical Meaning: Speed at which the envelope of a wave packet (or modulation) propagates.

12. Anisotropic and Dispersive Media

Anisotropic Media

Properties: Media where permittivity ε and permeability μ are tensor quantities.

Effect: Wave propagation depends on direction relative to the material's principal axes.

Dispersive Media

Frequency-Dependent Permittivity: $\varepsilon(\omega) = \varepsilon'(\omega) - j\varepsilon''(\omega)$

Implications: Phase and group velocities vary with frequency, affecting signal integrity.

13. Complex Wave Propagation

Complex Wave Vector

Definition: $\vec{k} = \vec{k'} - j\vec{k''}$

Interpretation:

  • $\vec{k'}$: Phase shift per unit length.
  • $\vec{k''}$: Attenuation per unit length.

Propagation in Complex Media

Exponentially Attenuated Waves: $\vec{E}(\vec{r}) = \vec{E_0}e^{-\vec{k''}\cdot\vec{r}}e^{j(\omega t-\vec{k'}\cdot\vec{r})}$

14. Examples and Applications

Wireless Communication

Free Space Propagation: Analysis of signal attenuation and phase changes over distance.

Remote Sensing and Radar

Reflection and Refraction: Understanding wave interactions with atmospheric layers and the Earth's surface.

Optical Fibers

Total Internal Reflection: Guiding light through dielectric waveguides based on critical angles.
